## Credibility under constraint

The story is written in a voice that is constantly negotiating credibility. The narrator anticipates disbelief and tries to preempt it. She offers caveats, jokes, and careful self-qualification.

P1. Credibility is not a stable attribute here; it is a social outcome. The narrator’s credibility depends on whether her audience accepts the authority of her husband and the medical frame he represents.

P2. The diary form complicates this. The narrator appears to write privately, yet she writes as if she were being overheard. That “as if” is the mark of surveillance.

## Voice as survival

The narrator’s voice does two things at once: it expresses distress, and it manages the consequences of expressing distress.

R1. When she softens a claim (“perhaps,” “I suppose,” “of course”), read it as strategy, not weakness.

R2. When she confides to the page, read it as risk management. Writing is both release and danger.

## The story’s ethical demand

The story does not ask you to diagnose the narrator. It asks you to examine the conditions under which a person’s testimony is discounted. The horror comes from a system in which the narrator’s words can be reclassified as symptoms.
