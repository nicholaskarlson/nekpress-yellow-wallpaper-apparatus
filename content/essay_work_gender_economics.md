## Dependence is an economy

The narrator’s dependence is not only emotional. It is economic. Control over work, time, and mobility produces a specific kind of vulnerability: the person who cannot earn or decide becomes the person who must persuade.

P1. The story depicts “rest” as the absence of productive labor, but the absence is imposed, not chosen. The narrator is not relieved; she is prevented.

P2. The household becomes a workplace where the narrator is both the worker and the work product: her health is managed, monitored, and evaluated.

## Gender norms as rules of speech

Certain speech acts are permitted: gratitude, humor, deference. Others are punished: insistence, anger, refusal. The narrator’s writing is an attempt to speak outside the permitted set.

R1. Track when she performs cheerfulness.

R2. Track when she hides frustration as “silliness.” That word often marks the boundary of what can be said.

## The cost of enforced idleness

When you remove meaningful work, you do not create peace. You create a vacuum. Attention pours into the remaining object. In the story, the wallpaper becomes that object. The fixation is not random; it is the predictable outcome of a life narrowed to a single room and a single schedule.
