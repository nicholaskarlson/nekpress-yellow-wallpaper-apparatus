## Why this story still reads as “clinical”

The narrator speaks in a register that is both intimate and constrained. She writes as if the page were a private room, but the page is also a witness: a place where she must be careful, persuasive, and occasionally evasive. That tension is the engine of the story.

P1. The text repeatedly asks you to decide what counts as evidence. A husband’s reassurance can sound like care or like management. A physician’s certainty can sound like expertise or like refusal. A room can be a refuge or a device.

P2. The story’s horror is not an external monster. It is the feeling of being rendered *legible* only on someone else’s terms. The narrator’s “symptoms” become the only language she is allowed to speak.

## A note on the ending

The ending is “cold” because it refuses the comfort of repair. The story does not present a tidy moral lesson; it presents an outcome that has the shape of a logic. The question is not “why did this happen?” but “what was made inevitable by the rules of the room?”

## How to use the apparatus

R1. Read the constraint ledger once, quickly, to see the main arc.

R2. Then use the scene companion for a second read. Each card is a prompt: a line to re-hear, a detail to track, a question to test.

R3. Use the notes sparingly. They are keyed to passages where the narrative makes a turn: from hope to bargaining, from bargaining to concealment, from concealment to fixation.
