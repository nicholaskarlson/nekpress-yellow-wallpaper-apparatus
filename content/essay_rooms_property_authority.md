## Rooms are legal and economic facts

A room is not just a setting. It is property. It can be assigned, restricted, and controlled. The narrator’s confinement is not accidental; it is permitted by ownership and by the social authority that comes with it.

P1. The story repeatedly treats the room as an object that can be described without moral content: windows, walls, floor, furniture. But the moral content arrives through use. Who chooses the room? Who can leave it? Who can change it?

## “For her comfort” as a property claim

Authority often presents itself as comfort. The room is selected “for her,” not by her. The schedule is designed “for her,” not with her. This is the grammar of dependence: decisions are made in your name while your agency is treated as a risk.

## Why the wallpaper matters as a surface

Wallpaper is a domestic technology. It turns a wall into something curated: pattern, taste, repetition. It can also become a record of time and contact. In the story, the wallpaper is the one thing the narrator can study without needing permission.

R1. Think of the wallpaper as an interface: a surface that invites attention and absorbs it.

R2. Think of it as a contract: the room’s design speaks a language of authority, and the narrator answers in the only language she is allowed—interpretation.

## Property and the ending

The ending is not a triumph over property. It is an exposure of property’s logic. When the room is treated as a device for control, the only “escape” available inside the system is to become illegible to it.
