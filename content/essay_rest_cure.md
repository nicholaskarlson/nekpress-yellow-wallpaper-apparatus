## A therapy that doubles as a theory of women

The so-called “rest cure” is not only a medical protocol. It is a story about what a person is *for*. The cure assumes that intellectual effort, ambition, and self-direction are sources of illness in certain bodies, and that health is identical with compliance.

P1. The central mechanism is deprivation framed as care. The patient is removed from work, writing, social exchange, and decision-making, and the removal is narrated as protection.

P2. The cure also depends on a moralized account of energy. If a woman is “nervous,” the explanation is not structural (labor, isolation, unequal authority) but personal (temperament, imagination). The cure therefore treats the social world as neutral and the patient as the problem.

## Domestic medicine and household governance

In the story, the household functions like a clinic. The doctor-husband’s expertise extends beyond the body into the environment: the room, the schedule, the visitors, the permissible activities. Medical judgment becomes a general right to decide.

R1. Notice how often the narrator anticipates the doctor’s evaluation. The medical voice becomes an internal critic.

R2. Notice how medical language collapses into everyday language: “sensible,” “practical,” “for your own good.” These are not neutral phrases. They are tools for making refusal sound irrational.

## What the story does with “health”

The story does not give you a clear clinical diagnosis. Instead, it shows how “health” can be used as a boundary: the healthy person is the one who agrees with the authority’s account of reality. The narrator’s writing threatens that boundary. It is evidence that she has a mind of her own.
